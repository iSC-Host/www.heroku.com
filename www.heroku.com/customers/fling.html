Share Life’s Moments with New Friends Around the World Using Randomized Messaging App Built on Heroku

With Fling – Message the World, users can send text, photos, or videos to up to fifty random recipients anywhere in the world.

A message in a bottle – the age-old image captures our imagination and evokes remarkable stories of people connecting through pure chance across oceans. We can imagine the inspired hope of the sender, the mysterious message itself, and the curiosity and delight of the recipient. Chance encounters with strangers in faraway places remind us of the beauty and power of human connection and make the world seem a little bit smaller. Nowadays, we can instantly cross oceans on our devices in thousands of ways. But can we experience the same storytelling magic of a message in a bottle?

Inspiration for an app called Fling came to Marco Nardone one day in January 2014 as he was crossing an ocean himself on a flight to Hong Kong and watching his plane’s progress on the real-time map. His vision was to take the ‘message in a bottle’ concept and amplify its impact – so senders can transmit their message to numerous random recipients at once, anywhere in the world. He wanted to be able to ‘fling’ a private message and literally watch it fly and land in multiple places on a world map. Not only would be experience be playful, but new friendships could arise based on common interests. As the founder of UK-based social network startup Unii, Ltd, Nardone took the idea back to his team and the Fling was born.

Random Acts of Messaging on Heroku

The Unii, Ltd. development team originally chose Heroku for their first app, Unii, primarily because they needed to get their app to market quickly. Unii had started as a responsive web app, but the company wanted to pivot towards a mobile-first approach with their products. When they began development on Fling, the team was just getting started working together on mobile apps and Fling was a side-project to hone their skills. Therefore it was natural for them to use Heroku to deploy and iterate Fling. With its fast-paced growth, Fling has since become a full-fledged product in their portfolio.

Fling is built on a distributed architecture – a fleet of apps provide different parts of the overall service and can be scaled differently. The native client-side apps were built using the iOS and Android toolkits. Fling’s main API was built using Ruby on Rails connected to a Heroku Postgres database, and the team used Erlang to create Fling’s chat system. A handful of Heroku Elements round out their implementation including Redis To Go for their Redis instance, Bonsai for Elasticsearch, RabbitMQ Bigwig for cloud messaging services, and Mandrill by Mailchimp for email delivery.

Nothing beats having a single slider that can help you instantly scale up dynos when you need more performance. Bilal Khan, Head of Product, Fling

Exponential growth is inherent in the app design, as users can send messages to up to 50 recipients. The Fling app needed to scale as quickly as it grew, so the team uses performance dynos to help them keep the app running at peak performance. Since it launched, Fling’s userbase has grown into the multi-millions, and billions of flings have circled the globe.

We couldn’t scale the main core of the app so effectively without performance dynos. They have really been a game changer. Bilal Khan, Head of Product, Fling

App Experience

Fling is as simple to use as it sounds. Using their iOS or Android device, a sender types in a message or uploads a photo or video, and selects the number of recipients for their message. When they hit send, they see an animated result that shows pins dropping on a map of the world indicating the general location of the message’s recipients. If a recipient likes the message, they can reply and initiate a chat. The experience is immediate and highly engaging, as well as inherently viral. The more you fling, the more responses you receive, and the more friends you make.

Who knows how many new far-flung friends await your message?
